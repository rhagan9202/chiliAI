# Medicaid

**Program Integrity Context**

Medicaid and CHIP provide free or low-cost health coverage to millions of people including low-income adults, children, pregnant women, the elderly adults, and people with disabilities. The Government Accountability Office (GAO) has included Medicaid on its list of high-risk programs since 2003, acknowledging that the size, complexity, and diversity of Medicaid make the program particularly challenging to oversee at the federal level.

Because Medicaid is a federal-state partnership, state Medicaid agencies and CMS share mutual obligations and accountability for the integrity of the program, including the development of program safeguards necessary to ensure proper and appropriate use of federal and state dollars. States provide the first line of defense against fraud, waste, and abuse in the Medicaid program and, in accordance with federal laws and guidance, establish eligibility standards and enrollment of beneficiaries, screen and enroll providers, establish payment policies, contracts with managed care plans (MCPs), and process claims for items and services furnished to Medicaid beneficiaries enrolled in Medicaid fee-for-service programs. In addition to providing federal funding to support each state’s Medicaid program, CMS also provides states with regulatory requirements and other guidance on federal Medicaid requirements; education and technical assistance, including tools and data; program assessment and feedback; and federal resources for strengthening their program integrity capacities.

The Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS), State Medicaid Agencies (SMAs), and Managed Care Plans (MCPs) share responsibility for program integrity in Medicaid. This division of responsibilities helps to build a continuum of activities that safeguards the program. However, this approach can also create fragmentation, as requirements are passed from one entity to the next. This may lead to increased risk of unimplemented controls, ineffective communication and coordination of program integrity activities, and loss due to fraud, waste, and abuse.

a) Dental/DMEPOS

Dental services represent an area of demonstrated risk in state Medicaid plans.[[1]](#footnote-1)

- OIG audits, inspections, reviews, and investigations have found the same billing errors repeated across dental providers, leading to duplicate billing and incorrect services claimed. The most common services affected by dental billing errors include tooth socket repairs and x-rays.
- Additionally, research indicates that the corporate practice of dentistry setting expectations and standards for higher utilization, along with the widespread practice of overtreatment for services including steel crowns and root canals may drive overutilization and medically unnecessary services. Past law enforcement investigations have frequently identified instances of dental, vision, and hearing care providers fraudulently billing Medicare, Medicaid, and private health plans for non-covered services disguised as covered services, services not medically necessary, and services not actually rendered. Dental providers were also found to produce claims for patient IDs that did not actually receive services.
- Commercial dental plans have begun to provide coverage for various teledentistry services. Patients can receive services in their homes, and dentists can provide virtual exams, prescribe medications, and refer patients to local dentists for further examination. Teledentistry may be represented among emerging telehealth fraud, waste, and abuse, in which practitioners are paid for telehealth services are that do not meet Medicaid coverage requirements.
- Past law-enforcement investigations have identified instances of dental, vision, and hearing providers billing Medicaid and private health plans for services that the provider was not properly certified or licensed to administer at the time of service. In addition to primary practitioners not having appropriate licensure, investigations have identified instances of dental clinics billing Medicaid for the provision of x-rays by dental assistants who were not appropriately certified, AND thus ineligible to bill the x-rays to Medicaid. State law and licensing requirements also commonly require various dental hygiene services be performed with the supervision and direction of a dentist. Medicaid has been impacted by improper billing for services that did not have appropriate dental provider supervision. Providers in dental care have also submitted claims that misidentified the treating practitioner which obscured the fact that the provider was not credentialed to provide care for the Medicaid and private dental insurance beneficiaries or Medicare beneficiaries receiving vision services.
- A past OIG review found dental services billed to dental providers did not always comply with certain Federal and State requirements. Specifically, the dentists in the practice who performed the services were not always enrolled in the Medicaid program. This brings into question the consistency of verification of enrollment status of performing dentists at dental practices.
- The inclusion of both independent and commercial suppliers and service providers providing access to dental devices, through in-house laboratories and dispensaries and insufficient controls guaranteeing the quality and medical necessity of prosthetic devices from these prosthetic device suppliers may represent an increased risk of improper payment.

***Risk Factors/ Indicators:*** Existing policies/controls may be insufficient to identify and prevent:

- Overbilling to Medicaid for overtreatment of patients beyond what is medically necessary in a single encounter.
- Overbilling to Medicaid for fees incurred from multiple encounters that should have been a single encounter.
- Fraudulent upcoding and other coding errors that bill for more expensive basic and major dental services than actually delivered.
- Fraudulent or incorrect billing of services not rendered or insufficient support of medical necessity of dental services.
- Billing for cosmetic surgeries/procedures that are inappropriately documented as medically necessary
- Emerging telehealth fraud, waste, and abuse including teledefrauding schemes
- Providers using beneficiary inducement schemes and concealing illegal kickbacks to physicians, marketers, and recruiters to increase referrals, enabling fraudulent billing and improper payments.
- Billing from providers with inadequate licensure or certification, or billing from providers with licenses previously suspended and designated as excluded from billing to the program.
- Improper billing by dental providers for x-rays performed by uncertified technicians.
- Improper billing by dental assistants for services that require supervision of a qualified dental provider.
- Self-referral and kickback risks stemming from the close care delivery and operational ties between the provider ordering the prosthetic device and the practitioner fabricating and dispensing the device in practices with in-house dental labs, optical dispensaries, and hearing aid dispensaries.
- Insufficient supplier enrollment controls and compliance activities may be insufficient to ensure that dental laboratories and technicians operate in a compliant manner and fabricate covered dental devices (e.g. dentures, crowns, bridges, implant crowns, etc.) that are safe, of sufficient quality, and custom fitted for the beneficiary.
- Inadequate MCO administration and oversight of dental benefits including overpayment, recovery, and utilization management activities (e.g. inadequate review prior-authorization requests to determine qualification for orthodontics including prerequisites for comprehensive orthodontic services (D8090), leading to unallowable payments ).

b) Vision / DMEPOS

- Past law-enforcement investigations have identified instances of vision services billed to Medicaid in which eyeglasses fitting and repair were billed as separate services when they should have been included within a single claim. Other investigations found Medicaid and private health plans billed for eyeglasses that were never provided to beneficiaries.
- Ophthalmologist and optometrist co-management arrangements represent an additional improper relationship risk from vision care providers. While these professional arrangements are not required to satisfy Stark Safe Harbor exceptions to exist, the nature of the co-management practice creates opportunities for improper billing involving illegal self-referral and fee-splitting.
- The State-specific variability around scope of practice may create challenges for CMS to ensure that optometrists are working in their scope of practice. The scope of practice for optometrists varies state-by-state around oral medication authority, oral steroid prescription authority, injectables authority, and lasers authority.

***Risk Factors/ Indicators:*** Existing policies/controls may be insufficient to identify and prevent:

- Payment of improper or duplicate vision care claims caused by billing errors.
- Upcoding and other coding errors that bill for more expensive vision services than actually delivered.
- Billing Medicaid for false or medically inappropriate eyeglasses and contact lenses
- Instances of vision services billed to Medicaid in which eyeglasses fitting and repair were billed as separate services when they should have been included within a single claim.
- Payment of fraudulent or incorrect billing, including forged or fabricated documentation for services not rendered or insufficient support of medical necessity of vision care services.
- Billing for cosmetic surgeries that are inappropriately documented as medically necessary vision surgery.
- Providers using beneficiary inducement schemes and concealing illegal kickbacks to physicians, marketers, and recruiters to increase referrals, enabling fraudulent billing and improper payments.
- Ophthalmologists/optometrists who are not operating in compliance with OIG guidance for appropriate co-management of patients.
- Payments to vision care providers who do not have proper licensure or credentialing and allowing unlicensed or otherwise unqualified staff to provide vision services. States may have varying standards on the procedures allowed to be covered if performed by an optician versus an optometrist.

c) Hearing / DMEPOS

- Past law enforcement investigations have identified instances of hearing care providers fraudulently providing ear buds and other nonqualifying items as covered and medically appropriate hearing aids and billing these false hearing aids to Medicaid and private health plans.
- Providers incorrectly test and overstate hearing loss to influence patients into buying more expensive hearing aids than necessary and thus overbilling the insurer.
- Free hearing tests commonly used to encourage beneficiaries to undergo audiologic services, as well as direct financial inducements, have been used as a mechanism to sell hearing aids to beneficiaries.
- New FDA regulations may open Medicaid to improper billing for OTC hearing aids and direct-to-consumer hearing aids sales based on orders from online or at-home screening tests that produce questionable assessment of medical necessity.

***Risk factors /Indicators:*** Existing policies/controls may be insufficient to identify and prevent:

- Improperly billed hearing aids.
- Fraudulent billing for services not rendered or based on forged documentation to support medical necessity of hearing care services.
- Claims payment for medically inappropriate hearing aids furnished by Direct-to-Consumer hearing aid suppliers based on orders generated from online or at-home FDA approved screening tests.
- Illegal kickbacks to physicians, marketers, and recruiters to increase referrals, as well as beneficiary inducement, enabling fraudulent billing and improper payments for hearing aids.
- Payments to hearing services providers who do not have proper licensure or credentialing and allowing unlicensed or otherwise unqualified staff to provide hearing services.
- Payment to fraudulent suppliers and ensure that enrolled hearing aid suppliers and personnel (e.g. hearing aid fitters) operate in a compliant manner and provide medically necessary custom fitted devices.

1) Nursing Facilities

The Medicaid program covers care in nursing facilities for eligible beneficiaries in need of skilled nursing services, rehabilitation services, or long-term care. Sections 1819 and 1919 of the Social Security Act (the Act) provide that nursing facilities participating in the Medicaid program must meet certain specified requirements (Federal participation requirements), including requirements related to quality of care, nursing services, and infection control. These sections also establish requirements for CMS and States to survey nursing facilities to determine whether they meet Federal participation requirements.

- Nursing Home Medicaid Kickback Schemes. Nursing facilities are vulnerable to many types of kickback schemes, including those seeking to pay financial incentives for steering beneficiaries to the nursing facility and schemes involving the receipt of kickbacks or benefits for prescribing specific treatment. For example, kickback arrangements may be disguised as payments for services provides including, for example, entry into “medical directorship agreements” with physicians over that purport to pay the physicians for administrative services, but instead are vehicles by which the physicians were paid to refer clients to the nursing facility.
- Beneficiary Abuse and Neglect. Nursing facilities must ensure that alleged violations involving mistreatment, abuse, or neglect, including injuries of unknown source, are reported immediately to the facility administrator and State survey agency. Nursing facility residents are at increased risk of abuse and neglect when health care professionals and caregivers fail to report abuse, or when incidents of potential abuse or neglect are not acted upon in a timely manner.
- Failure to meet minimum staffing requirements. Recent high-profile investigations have identified nursing facility fraud relating to falsifying records regarding meeting minimum requisite staffing levels to avoid sanctions and to continue to receive state and federal funding, all the while failing to provide residents with the level and quality of care they required. For example, in one case the facility falsified staffing information provided to the Pennsylvania Department of Health and CMS to show that the facilities were in compliance with the conditions of participation in Medicare and Medicaid. The false certifications—which included, for example, various employees adding the names of individuals who were not actually working, not providing direct patient care, and, in some cases, not even in the building, to staffing sheets to make it appear that the requisite minimum staffing ratio was being met—enabled the corporations to evade penalties for failing to provide sufficient staffing to meet the needs of the residents.
- Fraudulent Cost Reporting. A provider may include inappropriate expenses in claims made to Medicaid. These expenses often include the costs of items for personal consumption and use. For example, nursing home providers submit annual reports to the Medicaid program explaining how much it costs to take care of a nursing home resident. Only those costs that are related to resident care are allowable. Some providers fraudulently include personal costs, such as the remodeling of their own home, in the nursing home cost report and falsely describe it as work done at the nursing facility. The provider then submits a bill for an inflated rate for resident care based upon this false information.
- Related Entities. Caring for residents in long-term care facilities requires a spectrum of goods, services, and staff for nursing care, rehabilitative services, meals, building maintenance/cleaning, medical supplies, medication, operations management, and more. Often, nursing homes contract for all or some of these goods and services. In many instances, they contract with companies that share common ownership or control with the nursing home, which are known as related parties. Federal law recognizes that if a nursing home obtains goods or services from a business that is a separate legal entity, but that entity is owned or controlled by some or all of the nursing home owners, they are essentially obtaining these goods and services from themselves. Conducting business with related parties is legal and commonplace, but the practice creates the potential for significant fraud, waste, and abuse if unchecked. The risks created by related-party businesses include conflicts of interest and self-dealing by the owner. Also, the quality of care will suffer if funds intended for resident care are diverted to provide excessive profit to those with ownership and control.

***Risk Factors/ Indicators:*** Existing policies/controls may be insufficient to identify and prevent:

- False reporting and failure to maintain appropriate staff-to-resident ratios to ensure quality care.
- Misrepresentation of a patient’s diagnosis or condition and/or billing Medicaid for a service or product that is not medically necessary
- Billing for therapy services or visits that were not provided.
- Billing for more expensive services or more therapy units than were provided.
- Bills Medicaid for drugs dispensed without a lawful prescription
- Bills Medicaid for non-covered services by describing them as covered services
- Kickback schemes involving financial incentives paid to the nursing facility or staff by Medicaid health care providers or suppliers.
- Kickback schemes involving financial incentives paid for referrals to the facility.
- Employment of individuals with disqualifying backgrounds.
- Failure to report and investigate reported incidents of potential abuse or neglect to the State in accordance with Federal and State requirement
- Submission of cost reporting that fails to disclose related entities and other false statements or omitted material information regarding their transactions with related organizations
- Fraudulent self-dealing such as use of complex and improper real estate deals, excessive loans, inflated rents, and undisclosed fees paid to “related entities.
- Physical abuse of Medicaid patients or any patient in a Medicaid-funded facility. The resident has bruises, welts, lacerations, broken bones, burns, or injuries that are inconsistent with the explanation of the injuries.
- The provider claims to provide services, products, or tests for free.

## II. Medicaid Managed Care/ Beneficiary Eligibility and Enrollment

***Identification and Recovery of Improper Capitation Payments***

Improper capitation payments made for ineligible beneficiaries are generally due to:

- Eligibility determination errors, where the improper capitation payment is a byproduct of an error in eligibility determination, redetermination, or change that is processed inaccurately and inappropriately based on federal regulations and state-specific policies for Medicaid eligibility; or
- Data processing errors are payment errors that should have been avoided through the state’s Medicaid Management Information System (MMIS) or other payment system(s). Common causes of data processing errors include gaps in data transfer and systems communication issues, incorrect programming, missing or incorrect beneficiary information, and lack of edits in states’ claims processing systems.

State Medicaid Agencies are ultimately responsible for preventing, identifying, and returning the federal share of improper capitation payments. States use a variety of methods to prevent and identify ICPs for ineligible beneficiaries, including ongoing staff training regarding eligibility verification processes and data updates, information system matching and quality control reviews, and frequent data validation against outside state, federal and commercial databases to help identify beneficiary eligibility status changes and system discrepancies in order prevent and identify overpayments. In addition, states require that contracted MCPs report information learned about a change in beneficiary circumstances that might impact eligibility[[5]](#footnote-36), providing an additional avenue to identify changes in beneficiary status that require attention.

CMS provides additional federal oversight related to improper capitation payments through state program integrity reviews and audits, including PERM. Review of improper managed care capitation payments under the PERM process is limited solely to a review of enrollee information and capitation payments to identify data processing errors including, but are not limited to, managed care payment errors.[[6]](#footnote-37) Among the PERM Managed Care Data Processing errors identified in the review are those coded as ‘Non-covered Service/Beneficiary (DP2) errors’ which result primarily from capitation payments made by the state when the beneficiary did not have active Medicaid eligibility on the sampled date of service, resulting in an overpayment. This may be due to ineligibility for managed care because the beneficiary no longer had active eligibility for Medicaid during the period under review, or had passed away in a month prior to the capitation payment to the health plan.[[7]](#footnote-38)

While federal statute limits the collection of improper capitation payments due to eligibility determination errors[[8]](#footnote-39), states are subject to recoveries for overpayments that are identified because of a data processing error. The state is required to report and return the federal share of overpayments found in error from data processing on a claim-by-claim basis, within one year and in accordance with the statutory and regulatory requirements that govern recoveries of overpayments.[[9]](#footnote-40) This requirement applies without regard to whether the state recovers the improper capitation payment from the MCP.

***Risk Factors/Indicators:*** Risk factors identified as contributing to the Improper Capitated Payments vulnerability. These include:

1) States make improper capitation payments for beneficiaries concurrently enrolled in more than one state Medicaid program. This results in potential duplicate federal payments being paid to multiple states for the same beneficiary.
2) States make improper capitation payments to MCPs after a beneficiary’s death. Insufficient eligibility and enrollment information systems, data management processes, internal controls, data exchange and reporting requirements, and lead follow-up and information verification processes make it difficult to prevent improper capitation payments to MCPs after a beneficiary's death, and to ensure timely recovery of such overpayments.
3) States make duplicate capitated payments for a single beneficiary. Lacking data matching systems and internal controls hinder the MCPs’ ability to identify Medicaid enrollees who have been assigned multiple Medicaid Identification (ID) numbers, resulting in state and federal overpayments.
4) States’ data validation systems, data sharing capabilities, and internal controls insufficient to prevent improper payments. State data systems not adjusting previously made capitation payments based on retroactively reported eligibility status changes, state challenges in validating eligibility changes over time, and inconsistent data sharing across state eligibility system databases impact the ability to identify and prevent claims for federal match.

## III. Medicaid Managed Care / Provider Screening and Enrollment

**Medicaid managed care plan provider network and ineligible providers**

MCPs may not be consistently preventing improper claims payments associated with providers that are on administrative action lists (e.g., OIG’s List of Excluded Individuals and Entities (LEIE), Data Exchange System (DEX), Social Security Administration Death Master File (SSA DMF), GSA’s System for Award Management (SAM)). This includes payments for claims associated with deceased providers, providers with expired or revoked licenses, and providers excluded from Medicaid by federal or state oversight bodies. As a result, MCPs have continued to make improper claims payments to inappropriate providers, contributing to overpayments in Medicaid managed care and increased capitation rates year-over-year. State contracts that are not compliant with federal requirements, inconsistent MCP procedures for identifying inappropriate providers, and limited MCP access to federal or state datasets may contribute to this problem.

A recent OIG review found that nearly 11% of all terminated providers were inappropriately enrolled in a State Medicaid program or were associated with $50.3 million in Medicaid payments, posing risks to beneficiary safety and quality of care.[[10]](#footnote-41) The Social Security Act specifies that providers terminated from participation in federal healthcare programs shall be terminated from MCP participating provider networks.[[11]](#footnote-42) Federal regulations require that states, through MCP contracts, require providers to be enrolled in Medicaid in order to participate in the MCP network.[[12]](#footnote-43) Further, oversight reports have identified that states have paid millions of dollars in capitation payments to MCPs for claims associated with terminated providers, indicating a lack of state oversight to ensure MCPs do not allow terminated providers to participate in their networks.[[13]](#footnote-44) Root causes that may lead to the inclusion of terminated providers within MCP networks include:

- MCPs may include providers who are not enrolled within their networks increasing the risk of improper payments issued to providers who have been terminated for cause in Medicare or in another state’s Medicaid program. A 2020 OIG review found that 11% of terminated providers were still able to serve Medicaid beneficiaries and of 584 of the terminated providers reviewed, associated with $50.3 million in Medicaid payments, 412 were not enrolled in a state Medicaid program. This finding suggests that by implementing controls to mitigate MCP network provider enrollment risks, SMAs may also be able to make significant improvements in addressing non-compliance with federal statute and regulation barring payments for terminated providers.

One issue is that MCPs who have been delegated provider screening and enrollment functions may not comply with requirements, allowing ineligible providers to enroll and join MCP networks. To illustrate, California provides contracted MCPs with the option to develop and implement a managed care provider screening and enrollment process that is substantively equivalent to the SMA’s provider enrollment process. MCPs who elect to enroll providers through this process collect application data and supporting documentation from providers, conduct screening and verification activities, and execute the Medi-Cal Provider Agreement on behalf of the state. The SMA requires that the MCP make these enrollment records available upon request.[[14]](#footnote-46) As network providers may choose to enroll through either the SMA’s state-level enrollment process or through the MCP’s process (as a managed care only provider[[15]](#footnote-47)), the MCP may need to confirm enrollment through either the state or another CA MCP, and, if not enrolled, will need to execute the states enrollment process on behalf the SMA, through which the MCP must verify that the provider has not been terminated ‘for cause’ from Medicare or another state’s Medicaid program. (MCPs who subcontract these functions must ensure that subcontracts clearly outline all network provider eligibility and enrollment provisions and establish sufficient monitoring and oversight practices to confirm subcontractor compliance.)

- MCPs may not have sufficient data access and internal controls to identify and terminate ineligible providers from their network. (e.g. MCPs may not have sufficient processes to review provider terminations reported by other state Medicaid programs and Medicare revocations and reconcile with the state’s actively enrolled provider list, which may allow ineligible providers to continue receiving payments within the state’s Medicaid program.)
- MCPs may not have sufficient controls to identify and prevent payments made to ineligible network providers, contributing to risk for improper payments and non-compliance with federal statute and regulations prohibiting federal payments for ineligible providers
